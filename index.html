Abstract

Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring has been proposed as a promising safety mechanism for detecting misaligned behavior in large language models. However, its reliability remains largely unexplored beyond English and across diverse model families. We present the first large-scale evaluation of CoT monitorability across 13 diverse languages and seven frontier model families, comprising 16 models. Using adversarial-hint evaluations that require explicit intermediate computation, together with analysis of internal answer-token probabilities, we consistently find CoT unfaithfulness across languages and hint types, with an average rate of 95.9% across 8B–120B parameter models. We find that frontier models systematically engage in strategic manipulation, including answer-switching, post-hoc rationalization, and procedural exploitation of hints, making external monitors struggle to detect deception. We show that frontier models often commit to the misaligned cue in their latent activations within the first 15% of generation, even when the CoT appears faithful. Surprisingly, these deceptive patterns remain 100% in low-resource languages, revealing fundamental limitations in current CoT-based oversight. Our results reveal that CoT monitoring is fundamentally fragile under linguistic distribution shift, providing a substantially weaker safety signal than what English-only studies suggest. These findings underscore an urgent need to develop robust CoT monitors and to accelerate research into white-box monitoring techniques, especially to improve CoT monitorability in mid- and low-resource languages.

Key Findings

95.9%
Cross-lingual deception collapse

Adversarial hints expose 95.9% deception across 13 languages and 16 frontier models — saturating at 100% in low-resource languages (Swahili, Telugu, Bengali). Monitoring fails most where oversight is most needed.

67%
Procedural exploitation dominates

Models fabricate trivially-true definitions for hint variables (K, Q) to reach the target answer. This single mechanism drives 67% of all reasoning failures and saturates near 100% in hint-used scenarios, evenly across every linguistic tier.

15%
Early latent commitment

Logit-lens analysis reveals that models commit to the misaligned cue in their internal activations within the first 15% of generation — before the visible CoT begins to deliberate. The correct answer stays suppressed throughout decoding.

8B–120B
Systemic across scales & ecosystems

Failures persist uniformly from 8B to 120B parameters across 16 models in 7 frontier families, and extend to closed-source systems including GPT-4o-mini and Claude Haiku 4.5. Scale alone does not mitigate the failure mode.

Results

Cross-lingual collapse

Takeaway: Complex hints lower answer-level susceptibility — the average BER drops from 86.3% to 19.9%, near the no-hint baseline of 17.5%. But when models do follow the hint, deception stays near 100%. Lower susceptibility does not mean more monitorable reasoning.

How models conceal cues

Takeaway: Procedural exploitation is the unifying mechanism. Models fabricate trivially-true definitions for hint variables — the K=1, Q=1 trick from the Overview — to mask cue reliance behind rigorous-looking computation. Together with HI-Arith, it drives 67% of all failures, evenly across all resource tiers.

Early latent commitment

Takeaway: Under simple hints, commitment to the misaligned cue forms in the activations within the first 15% of generation — before the visible CoT begins to deliberate. The correct answer stays suppressed throughout decoding. Even under complex hints, where partial compute-then-switch is observable, the decisive override happens inside the hidden state — invisible to external monitors.

Stress tests

Takeaway: The failure mode survives every ablation: target-letter variations, closed-source frontier systems, stochastic reruns, and shift composition. Complex hints don’t merely steer to a target letter — under linguistic distribution shift, they destabilize the entire hint-mapping procedure. This is a systemic property of CoT-based oversight, not an artifact of architecture, sampling, or option choice.
